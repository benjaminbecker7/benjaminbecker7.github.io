A Little Bit About Myself

I Love to Work With Computers

Ever since I was 5 and I was given my first GameBoy Advance with a cartridge of Super Mario Bros. 3, I have been incredibly interested in how computers work. Their ability to perform complex calculations and make entire worlds on a screen continues to amaze and inspire me as a programmer.

My interest in computers was further amplified when I was in middle school and purchased a copy of Minecraft. I immediately fell in love with the simplicity and modifiability of the game. In high school, I began to use this game to continue to explore the world of computer programming, developing mods to change certain aspects of the game and creating my own public server on the internet from scratch that I used to play with my friends.

It was also in high school that I began to take computer science courses at my school. Through these courses I learned to develop applications in Visual Basic, C++, and Java. In my AP Computer Science class that I took in my senior year of high school, I was able to extend my knowledge of Java that I had gained from playing Minecraft to the world of general programming. After learning about the Java Swing API, and a little independent study of the Java API's used to grab HTML content from the internet, I was able to independently write an application that made it easier to run sessions of congressional debate for my speech and debate team (more on that later). It was also in my AP Computer Science class that I learned more about how to connect Java applications to SQL databases over a network. And so, for my final project for my class, I created a simulated book store application with a Java Swing front-end and an SQL back-end that could process logins, account creations, and transactions over a network. Needless to say, all of these experiences came together to earn me a 5 on the AP Computer Science exam, and an A in the class.

I Love to Study American Politics

I grew up in a very political family: my grandfather was the chairman of the local Democratic party and ran for Senate a couple times, and half of the people in my family were lawyers. Thus, many of my family's dinner table conversations would end up being about politics, whether it was about what President Bush was doing in the White House or how great it was that President Obama was in charge or how horrible and unbelievable it was that President Trump got elected into office.

But, my own personal interest in American politics did not start to truly foment until I started high school and joined the speech and debate team. Perhaps overly confident in my own abilities, I chose to compete in what many would consider to be the most complex form of speech and debate: congressional debate. If you do not know what that is, the competitors essentially participate in a sort of mock congress: students can submit bills for the chamber to debate, there are elections for a person who leads the "sessions" (called the presiding officer), and there is a set of esoteric rules and procedures that the contestants must follow throughout the competition. The competitors go back and forth advocating either for or against a bill, while the judges rank the competitors based on how well they think the competitors would fit into the actual United States Congress. When I started, I had no clue what I was doing. I had no clue how to form cogent arguments, I did not understand how parliamentary procedure works, and the legislative process was a mystery to me.

Nevertheless, I persisted. I networked with many bright and eloquent debaters to gain a group of people who would vote the way I wanted, I read and memorized books on parliamentary procedure, and I studied debate theory and American politics until I could essentially eat, sleep, and breathe congressional debate. In doing so, I became a powerful player on the national congressional debate stage. I started qualifying for National Championship tournaments my sophomore year, and by my senior year I was the first person from my school to ever qualify for and compete at the Tournament of Champions in congressional debate. I created a caucus of over 40 other congressional debaters from Pennsylvania that could determine the votes in tournaments across the state, and that caucus still exists to this day. And, perhaps most importantly, I gained numerous friendships and connections to incredibly smart and politically-minded peers all across the country. By doing all of this, I cemented a legacy in high school congressional debate that has continued to exist after my graduation.

This background led me to pursue my degree in political science as soon as I started school at Colgate University. In my freshman year, I ran for and was elected to be one of the 10 senators to represent my class in the school's Student Government Association. As a freshman senator, I served as a co-chair of the Government Affairs Committee, which is tasked with maintaining and updating the bylaws and constitution of the Student Government Association. In this position, which is normally reserved for upperclassmen, I pioneered initiatives to increase transparency between the Student Government Association and the student body by writing legislation requiring the Director of Communications of the Association to provide monthly updates to the student body on the activities of the Student Government Association. The next year, I was elected as the Speaker of the Senate, where I was able to use my knowledge of parliamentary procedure to efficiently run Senate meetings. Finally, as a senior, I was elected to be the Vice President of Communications for the Student Government Associations, and in that position I was able to continue to pursue my transparency initiatives.

I Love Being Able to Combine My Love of Computer Science with My Love of American Politics

The first opportunity I had to apply my skills in computer science to political research was when I participated Colgate's Washington, DC Study Group my sophomore year. Part of the program involved working in a part of American politics. So, I joined the Center for Political Accountability as a research intern. As a research intern, I was tasked with figuring out how to use the data on donations to political organizations over the past 10 years to show the distribution of donors among publicly-traded companies, privately-owned companies, individuals, PACs, trade associations, and other organizations. At first we did not know how we were going to do that, but then I came up with an idea to create unique category codes that we could list next to each donation record in the datasets and then aggregate the money in each category and display the numbers in pie charts. By doing this, we could get a solid picture of which groups were donating more to these organizations over the past 10 years.

We also came across the issue of how to go through the research as quickly as possible while maintaining the integrity of the data. Keep in mind, we were going through hundreds of thousands of donation records and there were only three working interns at the time. So, using my knowledge of Visual Basic from my high school computer science courses, I coded macros in Microsoft Excel that allowed for us to transfer our categorizations among our spreadsheets. This made it so that we could reduce redundant work in our datasets, and cut a job that was supposed to take many months down to just three months. I later went on to port this program to Python with a command-line user interface and a more robust data storage system, but the core of the project was created that semester in DC.

It was through this experience that I realized that I did not just want to be a political science, but that I also wanted to be able to continue to hone my coding skills. Thus, my junior year I added a major in computer science to my program. I went on to continue my work at the Center for Political Accountability, and was even promoted as the Center's first ever Werner Brandt Research Fellow. Alas, non-profit work is not the most lucrative of professions, and as I am set to graduate in May I am looking for a job as a software developer so that I can continue to develop (pun intended) my skills as a computer programmer and full-stack developer.
